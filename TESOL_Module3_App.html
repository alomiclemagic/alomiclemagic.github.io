üéØ The Anatomy of a Perfect ESL Lesson

Every great lesson follows a clear structure. Here's the proven framework:

1. Warm-Up / Lead-In (5-10 minutes)

Purpose: Activate prior knowledge, create interest, establish context

Example: Show a picture and ask "What do you see?" or play a quick game related to the topic

2. Learning Objective (2 minutes)

Purpose: Tell students what they'll learn today

Example: "By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use past tense verbs to talk about your weekend."

3. Presentation / Input (10-15 minutes)

Purpose: Introduce new language or concept with clear examples

Example: Show grammar on board, give examples, explain rules, demonstrate pronunciation

4. Guided Practice (10-15 minutes)

Purpose: Students practice with your support and correction

Example: Fill-in-the-blank exercises, controlled speaking activities, drilling

5. Independent Practice / Production (15-20 minutes)

Purpose: Students use language freely and creatively

Example: Role plays, discussions, writing tasks, projects

6. Review / Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

Purpose: Reinforce key points, check understanding, preview next lesson

Example: "What did we learn today?" Quick quiz, exit ticket

7. Homework / Extension (optional)

Purpose: Extend learning beyond the classroom

Example: Practice exercises, writing assignment, video to watch

‚è∞ Time Management Tips

  • Plan more than you think you'll need (it's easier to cut than to fill time)
  • Have a "backup activity" ready for fast finishers
  • Use a timer to stay on track
  • The most important is guided & independent practice - don't rush these!

üìù Writing SMART Learning Objectives

SMART = Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound

‚ùå Bad Objective: "Students will learn about past tense"

(Too vague - how will you know if they learned it?)

‚úÖ Good Objective: "By the end of this lesson, students will be able to write 5 sentences about their last vacation using past simple verbs correctly."

(Specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound!)

üéØ Scaffolding: Supporting Students to Success

Scaffolding means providing temporary support that helps students reach higher levels. Think of it like training wheels on a bicycle!

Scaffolding Techniques:

  • üîπ Modeling: Show them how to do it first
  • üîπ Sentence Starters: Give them the beginning: "In my opinion..."
  • üîπ Word Banks: Provide vocabulary they might need
  • üîπ Visual Aids: Pictures, charts, diagrams
  • üîπ Pair Work: Let stronger students help weaker ones
  • üîπ Simplify Language: Use easier words to explain harder concepts
