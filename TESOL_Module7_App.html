Key Principles for Teaching Speaking

üéØ Fluency vs. Accuracy

Fluency: Speaking smoothly and confidently (doesn't have to be perfect)

Accuracy: Using correct grammar, vocabulary, pronunciation

Balance both! Fluency activities = free speaking, don't interrupt. Accuracy activities = controlled practice, correct mistakes.

üí™ Building Confidence
  • Create safe environment for mistakes
  • Start with pair work (less scary than whole class)
  • Give think time before speaking
  • Praise effort, not just perfection
  • Use sentence starters for support

Common Speaking Challenges & Solutions

Problem: Students won't speak

Solutions:

  • Start with pair work (less intimidating)
  • Use sentence starters
  • Give thinking time
  • Make topics interesting and relevant
  • Never force, always encourage

Problem: One student dominates

Solutions:

  • "Thank you, Maria. Now let's hear from..."
  • Assign roles (timekeeper, recorder, speaker)
  • Use "talking tokens" (each student gets 3)
  • Do round-robin activities

üéØ The Speaking Secret

Students need a REASON to speak. "Practice speaking" isn't motivating. But "convince your partner," "find the differences," "solve this mystery" - THAT'S motivating! Make speaking purposeful and meaningful! üí¨

The Three Stages of Listening

1Ô∏è‚É£ Pre-Listening

Purpose: Activate knowledge, introduce vocabulary, set purpose

Activities: Predict what they'll hear, discuss topic, pre-teach key vocabulary

2Ô∏è‚É£ While-Listening

Purpose: Focus attention, comprehend main ideas and details

Activities: Fill in blanks, answer questions, check predictions, take notes

Tip: Play audio 2-3 times! First = gist, Second = details, Third = check

3Ô∏è‚É£ Post-Listening

Purpose: Respond, discuss, extend learning

Activities: Discuss content, give opinions, role play, write summary

üéØ Scanning

üìö Intensive Reading

The Three Stages of Reading

1Ô∏è‚É£ Pre-Reading

  • Activate prior knowledge: "What do you know about...?"
  • Pre-teach difficult vocabulary
  • Look at title, pictures: "What do you think this is about?"
  • Set purpose: "Read to find out..."

2Ô∏è‚É£ While-Reading

  • Read for gist first, then details
  • Answer comprehension questions
  • Identify main ideas and supporting details
  • Work on vocabulary in context

3Ô∏è‚É£ Post-Reading

  • Discuss: "What did you think?"
  • Personal response: "Have you ever...?"
  • Extension activities: role play, write, present

‚úçÔ∏è Teaching Writing

Guide students through the writing process!

The Writing Process

1Ô∏è‚É£ Pre-Writing (Planning)
  • Brainstorm ideas
  • Make outlines or mind maps
  • Discuss with partners
  • Organize thoughts
2Ô∏è‚É£ Drafting
  • Write first draft
  • Focus on IDEAS, not perfection
  • Get thoughts on paper
  • Don't worry about mistakes yet
3Ô∏è‚É£ Revising
  • Read and improve CONTENT
  • Add details, delete unnecessary parts
  • Reorganize if needed
  • Peer feedback
4Ô∏è‚É£ Editing
  • Now fix grammar, spelling, punctuation
  • Check sentence structure
  • Use editing checklist
5Ô∏è‚É£ Publishing
  • Write/type final version
  • Share with class
  • Display on board or online
  • Celebrate!

üìù Essays

üìñ Stories

üì∞ Reports

üí¨ Reviews

üìã Instructions

How-to guides, recipes, directions.

Error Correction Strategies

üéØ When to Correct

  • During Drafting: DON'T correct! Let ideas flow.
  • During Revising: Focus on content, organization.
  • During Editing: NOW correct grammar, spelling.

üéØ How to Correct

  • Selective Correction: Don't mark EVERY error! Choose one type to focus on.
  • Error Codes: WW (wrong word), Sp (spelling), T (tense), etc.
  • Self-Correction: Mark errors, students find and fix them.
  • Peer Editing: Partners check each other's work.
  • Positive Feedback: Always note what's GOOD too!

‚úçÔ∏è The Writing Secret

Writing is HARD - even in your native language! Don't expect perfection. Focus on PROCESS over product. Celebrate progress! And remember: Students become writers by WRITING - regularly, frequently, on topics they care about! üìù

üîÑ Integrating the Four Skills

In real life, skills aren't separate - they work together!

üí° Why Integration Matters

Think about a conversation: You LISTEN to someone, SPEAK your response, maybe READ a text they send, and WRITE a reply. Real communication integrates all four skills naturally. Good ESL lessons do the same!

Examples of Integrated Lessons

üì∞ News Article Lesson
  1. LISTENING: Watch a news clip
  2. SPEAKING: Discuss the news with partner
  3. READING: Read detailed article on same topic
  4. WRITING: Write opinion paragraph

Result: All four skills practiced around one theme!

üé¨ Movie-Based Lesson
  1. READING: Read movie synopsis
  2. LISTENING & SPEAKING: Watch scene, discuss
  3. WRITING: Write review or alternative ending
  4. SPEAKING: Present to class
üì± Social Media Project
  1. READING: Read example posts
  2. WRITING: Create own posts
  3. SPEAKING: Present posts to class
  4. LISTENING: Listen to classmates' presentations

üîÑ Integration = Real Communication

The best ESL lessons feel like COMMUNICATION, not "skill practice." When students are engaged in meaningful tasks - discussing, creating, problem-solving - they naturally use all four skills without even realizing it. That's when real learning happens! üåü
