1Ô∏è‚É£ Communicative Language Teaching (CLT)

Core Principle: Language is for communication, not just grammar rules!

Key Features:

  • Focus on meaning over form
  • Real-life communication scenarios
  • Lots of speaking and interaction
  • Grammar taught in context, not isolation
  • Teacher as facilitator, students as communicators
‚úÖ Pros
  • Students learn to actually USE English
  • Engaging and motivating
  • Prepares for real-world communication
  • Develops fluency
‚ùå Cons
  • Can neglect accuracy/grammar
  • Requires confident teacher
  • Hard with very large classes
  • Some cultures resist speaking up

Best For: Adults, teenagers, conversation classes, speaking practice

2Ô∏è‚É£ Task-Based Learning (TBL)

Core Principle: Learn language by doing meaningful tasks!

Key Features:

  • Students complete real-world tasks (planning a trip, solving a problem)
  • Language emerges from the task itself
  • Three stages: Pre-task, Task, Language Focus
  • Student-centered learning
‚úÖ Pros
  • Highly motivating
  • Real-world relevance
  • Natural language use
  • Develops problem-solving
‚ùå Cons
  • Requires planning and materials
  • Can be noisy/chaotic
  • Hard to control language outcome
  • Assessment can be tricky

Best For: Intermediate to advanced students, project-based classes, business English

3Ô∏è‚É£ Total Physical Response (TPR)

Core Principle: Learn language through physical movement!

Key Features:

  • Teacher gives commands, students perform actions
  • No pressure to speak initially
  • Learning through doing and movement
  • Low-stress, fun approach
‚úÖ Pros
  • Perfect for absolute beginners
  • Great for young learners
  • Low-anxiety learning
  • Memorable and fun
‚ùå Cons
  • Limited to action verbs
  • Doesn't teach abstract concepts
  • Can feel childish for adults
  • Limited for advanced levels

Best For: Young learners (ages 3-10), absolute beginners, teaching vocabulary

4Ô∏è‚É£ The Direct Method

Core Principle: Teach ONLY in English, no translation!

Key Features:

  • All instruction in target language
  • Use gestures, pictures, demonstration
  • Grammar taught inductively (students discover rules)
  • Emphasis on speaking and listening first
‚úÖ Pros
  • Maximum English exposure
  • Natural immersion
  • Develops thinking in English
  • Good pronunciation focus
‚ùå Cons
  • Slow for abstract concepts
  • Can frustrate beginners
  • Time-consuming explanations
  • Some things NEED translation

Best For: Immersion programs, dedicated language schools, motivated students

5Ô∏è‚É£ Grammar-Translation Method

Core Principle: Understand grammar rules, translate texts

Key Features:

  • Explicit grammar instruction
  • Translation exercises
  • Reading and writing focus
  • Native language used freely
‚úÖ Pros
  • Clear, systematic structure
  • Good for grammar understanding
  • Works with large classes
  • Comfortable for shy students
‚ùå Cons
  • Little speaking practice
  • Doesn't develop fluency
  • Can be boring
  • Not communicative

Best For: Academic contexts, exam preparation, grammar-focused students

6Ô∏è‚É£ The Natural Approach

Core Principle: Acquire language naturally like children do!

Key Features:

  • Focus on input (listening, reading)
  • No forced speaking in early stages
  • Meaning over form
  • Low-anxiety environment
‚úÖ Pros
  • Low-stress learning
  • Natural development
  • Strong listening skills
  • Intrinsic motivation
‚ùå Cons
  • Very slow progress
  • Accuracy issues
  • Not suitable for time pressure
  • Requires massive input

Best For: Long-term learning, immersion contexts, young learners

üéØ How to Choose the Right Method

  • Student Age: Young = TPR, Teens/Adults = CLT/TBL
  • Student Goal: Communication = CLT, Exams = Grammar-Translation + CLT
  • Class Size: Large = Grammar-Translation, Small = CLT/TBL
  • Student Level: Beginner = TPR/Direct, Advanced = TBL/CLT
  • BEST APPROACH: Use a combination based on the situation!

üéØ 25 Communicative Activities Bank

Ready-to-use activities for each methodology!

CLT Activities (Communication Focus)

1. Information Gap Activities

Partner A has some information, Partner B has different information. They must communicate to complete the task.

Example: One student has a train schedule, the other needs to plan a trip.

2. Role Plays

Students act out real-life scenarios.

Example: "You're at a restaurant. Order food." "You're at the doctor. Explain your symptoms."

3. Discussion Circles

Students discuss opinions on topics.

Example: "Should everyone learn English?" "Is social media good or bad?"

4. Find Someone Who...

Students mingle and ask questions to find classmates who match criteria.

Example: "Find someone who has been to Paris" "Find someone who can play guitar"

5. Picture Differences

Partners have similar but slightly different pictures. They describe to find the differences.

TPR Activities (Movement Focus)

6. Simon Says

Classic game: "Simon says touch your nose!" Students only move when you say "Simon says."

7. Action Songs

Sing songs with actions (Head, Shoulders, Knees and Toes; If You're Happy and You Know It)

8. Command Chains

"Stand up, walk to the door, knock three times, sit down." Students perform the sequence.

TBL Activities (Task Focus)

9. Plan a Party

Groups plan a class party: budget, food, music, invitations. Present their plan.

10. Design a City

Create an ideal city: where to put schools, parks, shops. Draw map and present.

11. Solve a Mystery

Give clues, students work in groups to solve who committed a (fictional) crime.

üí° Activity Selection Tips

  • Have clear instructions ready
  • Model the activity first
  • Set time limits
