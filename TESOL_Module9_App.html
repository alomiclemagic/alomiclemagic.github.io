üí° Assessment is About Growth

The PURPOSE of assessment isn't to judge or grade - it's to INFORM teaching and SUPPORT learning. Good assessment shows students what they've mastered, what they're improving, and what needs more work. It's a tool for GROWTH, not punishment!

üìã Types of Assessment

Understanding different assessment purposes!

1Ô∏è‚É£ Diagnostic Assessment

Purpose: Find out what students already know BEFORE teaching

When: Beginning of course or unit

Examples:

  • Placement tests
  • Pre-tests before starting new topic
  • "What do you already know about..." activities

Why it matters: Helps you start at the right level!

2Ô∏è‚É£ Formative Assessment

Purpose: Check understanding DURING learning (ongoing)

When: Throughout the lesson/unit

Examples:

  • Exit tickets ("Write one thing you learned today")
  • Quick comprehension checks
  • Observation during activities
  • Thumbs up/down understanding check
  • Practice exercises

Why it matters: Lets you adjust teaching while there's still time!

3Ô∏è‚É£ Summative Assessment

Purpose: Evaluate what students learned AFTER teaching

When: End of unit, semester, course

Examples:

  • Final exams
  • Unit tests
  • Final projects
  • Presentations
  • Portfolios

Why it matters: Shows overall achievement and mastery!

üéØ The Assessment Balance

Good teachers use ALL THREE types!
‚Ä¢ Diagnostic = Where are we starting?
‚Ä¢ Formative = Are we making progress? (Most important! Frequent!)
‚Ä¢ Summative = What did we accomplish?

Formal vs. Informal Assessment

Formal Assessment
  • Tests & quizzes
  • Graded assignments
  • Standardized tests
  • Final projects
  • Planned & structured
Informal Assessment
  • Observation
  • Questions during class
  • Class discussions
  • Student questions
  • Spontaneous & ongoing

üìä Assessment Frequency

Formative assessment should happen EVERY CLASS! Quick checks, observations, questions - constantly monitor understanding. Summative assessment? Once per unit or semester. Think of it like this: Formative = GPS checking your route constantly. Summative = Did you arrive at the destination? üó∫Ô∏è

‚úèÔ∏è Creating Effective Tests & Quizzes

Design assessments that actually measure learning!

Types of Test Questions

1. Multiple Choice

Good for: Vocabulary, grammar, reading comprehension

Example: "She _____ to school every day. A) go B) goes C) going D) went"

Tips: Make all options plausible. Avoid "all of the above."

2. Fill in the Blank

Good for: Grammar, vocabulary in context

Example: "Yesterday I _____ (go) to the store."

Tips: Make sure there's only ONE correct answer!

3. True/False

Good for: Quick comprehension checks

Example: "The capital of France is Paris. T / F"

Tips: Avoid trick questions. Be clear and unambiguous.

4. Short Answer

Good for: Comprehension, opinions, explanations

Example: "Why did the character make that decision?"

Tips: Be specific about length expected (1 sentence? 1 paragraph?)

5. Essay/Extended Response

Good for: Writing skills, critical thinking, synthesis

Example: "Write about your most memorable vacation. (200 words)"

Tips: Provide clear rubric so students know expectations!

Rubrics: Your Assessment Superpower!

What is a Rubric?

A rubric is a SCORING GUIDE that clearly defines criteria and different levels of quality. It makes grading consistent, fair, and transparent!

Sample Writing Rubric

Criteria 4 - Excellent 3 - Good 2 - Needs Work 1 - Poor
Content Clear, relevant, detailed ideas Clear ideas, some details Unclear or off-topic Very unclear or missing
Organization Logical flow, clear paragraphs Mostly organized Some organization issues Confusing structure
Grammar Very few errors Some errors, doesn't affect meaning Many errors, affects meaning Too many errors to understand
Vocabulary Varied, appropriate words Adequate vocabulary Limited vocabulary Very basic or wrong words

‚úèÔ∏è Test Creation Tips

  • Test what you taught! (Don't surprise students)
  • Mix question types (variety keeps it interesting)
  • Start easier, get harder (builds confidence)
  • Clear instructions! ("Circle the correct answer")
  • Proofread! (Typos undermine credibility)
  • Time appropriate (Don't make it too long)

üí¨ Giving Powerful Feedback

Feedback that motivates and improves!

The Feedback Sandwich (But Better!)

Traditional "Sandwich" Method:

1Ô∏è‚É£ Positive: "Great job on your essay!"

2Ô∏è‚É£ Negative: "But your grammar needs work."

3Ô∏è‚É£ Positive: "Keep trying!"

Problem: Feels fake. Students see through it!

BETTER Approach: Specific + Actionable

‚ùå Vague: "Good job!"

‚úÖ Specific: "Your introduction clearly states your main idea. Great!"

‚ùå Just Criticism: "Your grammar is bad."

‚úÖ Specific + Actionable: "Watch verb tenses - you switched from past to present. Try: 'Yesterday I went (not go) to the store.'"

‚ùå Overwhelming: "You have 47 errors."

‚úÖ Focused: "Let's focus on article usage this week. I've marked 5 examples - can you fix them?"

The Power of "Yet"

Growth Mindset Language:

‚ùå "You can't do this."

‚úÖ "You can't do this YET - but you're improving!"


‚ùå "You're bad at pronunciation."

‚úÖ "Your pronunciation is improving! Keep practicing the 'th' sound."


The word "yet" is magical! It implies that success is coming with effort.

Written vs. Oral Feedback

‚úçÔ∏è Written Feedback

Best for:

  • Grammar errors
  • Detailed explanations
  • Students can refer back

Tips: Use symbols (Sp=spelling, T=tense) so it's not overwhelming!

üó£Ô∏è Oral Feedback

Best for:

  • Speaking activities
  • Immediate correction
  • Personal connection

Tips: Be encouraging! Focus on communication first, accuracy second.

Peer Feedback

Students Can Give Feedback Too!

How to do it:

  1. Give clear criteria ("Check if they have a clear topic sentence")
  2. Model first (show how to give good feedback)
  3. Use structured forms (checklist or questions)
  4. Focus on specific, kind, helpful comments

Benefits: Students learn by evaluating others. Plus it takes pressure off you!

üí¨ The Feedback Secret

The BEST feedback is: 1) Specific (not "good job"), 2) Timely (given soon after), 3) Actionable (student knows what to DO), 4) Encouraging (focuses on growth). Remember: Feedback is FOR students, not AT them. Your goal is to help them IMPROVE, not just judge them! üìà

Standardized Tests Overview

Major ESL Proficiency Tests:

TOEFL: Test of English as a Foreign Language

Used for: University admission (mainly US)

Format: Reading, Listening, Speaking, Writing

IELTS: International English Language Testing System

Used for: University admission, immigration (UK, Australia, Canada)

Format: Reading, Listening, Speaking, Writing

Cambridge Exams: PET, FCE, CAE, CPE

Used for: General proficiency certificates

Format: Multiple levels from beginner to proficient

üìä The Tracking Secret
