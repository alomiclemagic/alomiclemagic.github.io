## Course Overview
- **Target Age:** 8-16 years old

## üìö LESSON PLAN 1: MONEY BASICS (Week 1)

### Objectives
- Students understand what money is and its purpose
- Students learn the fundamental money rule: "Spend less than you make"
- Students recognize why financial literacy matters

### Duration: 2-3 class periods (45-60 minutes each)

### Materials Needed
- Whiteboard/markers
- "What is Money?" worksheet
- Real coins and bills (optional)
- Scenario cards

### Lesson Activities

#### Activity 1: What is Money? (15 minutes)
1. Ask students: "What is money?"
2. Discuss: Money = permission slips for stuff
3. Show real coins/bills
4. Discuss history briefly: Trading chickens ‚Üí coins ‚Üí paper ‚Üí digital

#### Activity 2: Why Money Matters (15 minutes)
- Have students list 5 things they want
- Discuss: Which require money?
- Create a class chart: Dreams vs. Cost

#### Activity 3: The Golden Rule (15 minutes)
- Introduce: "Spend Less Than You Make"
- Give examples:
  - If you earn $10 and spend $8, you WIN
  - If you earn $10 and spend $12, you LOSE
- Practice: Simple math problems with spending scenarios

#### Activity 4: Personal Reflection (10 minutes)
- Students complete: "Money Matters to Me" worksheet
- Share 2-3 responses with class

### Homework Assignment
Complete "Money Basics Quiz 1" (see assessment section)

### Extension Activities
- Create a class "Money Wish Board"
- Invite a local banker to speak (optional)
- Watch a 5-minute video about money history

## üìö LESSON PLAN 2: GETTING MONEY (Week 2-3)

### Objectives
- Students identify age-appropriate money-making opportunities
- Students understand the difference between allowance and earned money
- Students recognize their own earning potential

### Duration: 3-4 class periods

### Materials Needed
- "Income Sources" worksheet
- Scenario cards for different ages
- Role-play props (optional)
- Job market research sheets

### Lesson Activities

#### Activity 1: Types of Income (20 minutes)
1. Explain: Allowance vs. Chores vs. Jobs
2. Discuss pros/cons of each:
   - Allowance: Guaranteed but limited
   - Extra chores: Earn more, still at home
   - Real jobs: Most money, more responsibility

#### Activity 2: Age-Appropriate Opportunities (25 minutes)
**For Ages 8-10:**
- Allowance
- Extra chores
- Birthday money

**For Ages 11-13:**
- All of above PLUS:
- Babysitting
- Yard work
- Tutoring
- Selling items

**For Ages 14-16:**
- All of above PLUS:
- Part-time jobs
- Freelance work online
- Entrepreneurship

#### Activity 3: Planning Your Income (20 minutes)
- Students complete: "My Money-Making Plan" worksheet
- They choose 2-3 methods they could use
- Calculate potential monthly earnings

#### Activity 4: Research Project (ongoing)
- Students research one job/opportunity they're interested in
- How much it pays
- What skills needed
- Where to find these opportunities

### Homework Assignment
"Money-Making Research" worksheet + Interview a parent about how they earn money

### Extension Activities
- Invite someone with a unique job to speak
- Mock job interviews
- Create a "Jobs Fair" poster

## üìö LESSON PLAN 3: SAVING MONEY (Week 4)

### Objectives
- Students understand the power of saving
- Students learn different saving methods
- Students set and track savings goals

### Duration: 3 class periods

### Materials Needed
- Piggy bank or jar (bring one to show)
- Savings tracker worksheet
- Goal-setting worksheet
- Banking brochures (optional)

### Lesson Activities

#### Activity 1: Why Saving Matters (15 minutes)
- Tell story: "The $30 Friend"
- Student gets $5 allowance weekly
- Friend spends it all immediately
- Student saves it
- After 6 weeks: Friend has $0, Student has $30

#### Activity 2: Piggy Bank System (20 minutes)
- Show physical piggy bank
- Demonstrate saving process
- Calculate: If you save $5/week for 3 months = $60
- Discuss: What could $60 buy?

#### Activity 3: The 50/30/20 Rule (25 minutes)
- Teach the rule:
  - 50% Save
  - 30% Spend (fun)
  - 20% Save (big dreams)
- Give examples with different income amounts
- Students practice with sample incomes

#### Activity 4: Goal Setting (15 minutes)
- Students complete: "My Savings Goals" worksheet
- SHORT TERM (1-3 months)
- MEDIUM TERM (3-6 months)
- LONG TERM (1+ year)
- Calculate: How long to reach each goal

### Group Activity: Savings Challenge
- Class competition: Who can save the most this month?
- Share progress weekly
- Winner gets recognition (not money!)

### Homework Assignment
Start tracking your own savings this week + complete savings math problems

### Extension Activities
- Visit a local bank (virtual or in-person)
- Calculate compound interest (intro to algebra)
- Research savings accounts for kids

## üìö LESSON PLAN 4: SPENDING SMART (Week 5)

### Objectives
- Students distinguish between wants and needs
- Students apply decision-making strategies
- Students understand impulse buying

### Duration: 3-4 class periods

### Materials Needed
- "Want vs. Need" sorting cards
- Real product advertisements (print from online)
- 24-Hour Rule worksheet
- Price comparison sheets

### Lesson Activities

#### Activity 1: Want vs. Need (20 minutes)
- Define:
  - NEEDS: Food, clothes, shelter, school, medicine
  - WANTS: Games, snacks, cool shoes, etc.
- Give 20 examples
- Students sort them (game/worksheet)
- Discuss gray areas

#### Activity 2: The Brain Trick (15 minutes)
- Explain: Brain tries to turn wants into needs
- Examples:
  - "I NEED those sneakers" (No, yours work)
  - "I NEED that game" (No, but it's fun)
- Discuss advertising tricks
- How companies make us think we need things

#### Activity 3: The 24-Hour Rule (20 minutes)
- Introduce the rule: Wait 24 hours before buying
- What happens:
  - Forget about it
  - Realize you don't need it
  - Keep your money
- Practice with scenario: "You see a $60 speaker"

#### Activity 4: Price Comparison Game (25 minutes)
- Give students 5 products to "buy"
- They research prices at different stores
- Calculate savings
- Award points for finding best deals
- Make it competitive and fun

#### Activity 5: Cost Per Use Math (15 minutes)
- Teach the formula
- Examples:
  - Gaming headset: $80 √∑ 730 days = $0.11/day ‚úÖ
  - Fancy shoes: $120 √∑ 2 wears = $60/wear ‚ùå
- Students practice with their own purchases

### Homework Assignment
"Smart Spending Scenarios" worksheet + Real-world price comparison assignment

### Extension Activities
- Create anti-advertising posters
- Analyze real ads critically
- Budget exercise: You have $50, plan your month

## üìö LESSON PLAN 5: COOL MONEY FACTS & APPLICATIONS (Week 6)

### Objectives
- Students understand advanced money concepts
- Students apply learning to real situations
- Students think long-term about finances

### Duration: 2-3 class periods

### Materials Needed
- Interest calculation sheets
- Inflation explanation graphic
- Timeline activity sheets
- Real-world scenario cards

### Lesson Activities

#### Activity 1: Compound Interest is Magic (20 minutes)
- Show calculation:
  - Save $5/month from age 10 ‚Üí age 18 = $480+
  - Save $5/month from age 20 ‚Üí age 30 = less
- Discuss: Why start young?
- Use calculator to show compound growth
- Visual timeline of growth

#### Activity 2: Inflation Explained (15 minutes)
- Show: Same candy cost $0.50 (20 years ago)
- Now costs $1.50
- Money doesn't stay the same value
- Why grandparents say "back in my day"
- Current real examples (gas prices, games, etc.)

#### Activity 3: Credit Cards Aren't Magic (15 minutes)
- Explain: Borrowed money with interest
- Why parents stress about them
- Dangers of debt
- Good vs. bad use of credit

#### Activity 4: Rich People Secrets (15 minutes)
- Common misconceptions: Rich = smart
- The truth: Rich people spend LESS, not more
- Habits of financially successful people
- Case studies (optional)

### Group Discussion: Future Planning (20 minutes)
- What will cost money in the future?
- College
- Car
- Apartment
- Trips
- How can you prepare now?

### Homework Assignment
"Money Facts Challenge Quiz" + Future planning worksheet

### Extension Activities
- Research a local business owner
- Calculate your lifetime earnings potential
- Create a 10-year financial plan

## üìù Classroom Management Tips

1. **Make it relevant:** Use current trends, games, apps students know
2. **Be interactive:** Avoid lectures; use activities and discussions
3. **Celebrate progress:** Acknowledge effort and improvement
4. **Connect to real life:** Always explain "Why this matters to YOU"
5. **Differentiate:** Adjust activities for advanced/struggling students
6. **Make it fun:** Use games, competitions, role-plays
7. **Be inclusive:** Everyone has money conversations in their families

## üìö Differentiation Strategies

### For Younger Students (8-10)
- Simpler numbers
- Concrete examples (their piggy bank)
- Shorter activities
- More visual aids
- Less writing, more discussion

### For Older Students (13-16)
- Complex calculations
- Real-world scenarios
- Research components
- Written reflections
- Career connections

### For Struggling Learners
- Paired activities
- Simplified worksheets
- More visual supports
- One-on-one check-ins
- Use real money when possible

### For Advanced Learners
- Extended challenges
- Research projects
- Teach-back activities
- Real investments (simulator)
- Mentoring younger students

## üí° Common Questions & Answers for Teachers

**Q: Isn't this too advanced for 8-year-olds?**
A: Start simple! Focus on allowance and saving. Complex concepts come later.

**Q: How do I handle different family economic situations?**
A: Be sensitive. Use "earning money" language, not "family wealth." Everyone has value to offer.

**Q: What if students don't have allowance?**
A: Focus on chores, gifts, and other earning opportunities.

**Q: Should I teach about credit cards?**
A: At basic level yes, but in-depth for older students only.

**Q: How long should this course be?**
A: Flexible! 6-8 weeks standard, but can be compressed or extended.
